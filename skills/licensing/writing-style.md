# Brian Anderson — Email Writing Style

_Derived from analysis of 382 sent emails (Aug 2025 – Mar 2026). Use as boilerplate guidance._

---

## Tone
- Direct, professional, never stiff. Gets to the point without preamble.
- Confident but not pushy. Transparent about constraints ("we require partners to onboard first before...").
- No hollow affirmations ("Great question!", "Hope this finds you well"). Skips straight to substance.
- Conversational but economical — zero filler.

## Salutations
- First name only, followed by a **colon** (not a comma): `Jess:`, `Ashley:`, `Hello Tom:`
- For informal/internal: `Hi [Name],`
- For very short replies (1-2 sentences): often no greeting at all
- Cold outreach: `Hi [Name],` with no last name

## Sign-offs
- Standard: `Best,\nBrian`
- Cold outreach or slightly warmer: `Very best,\nBrian`
- Quick/casual reply: `-Brian` or `-B`
- No sign-off at all for the shortest replies (one-liners)

## Sentence & Paragraph Structure
- Short sentences. Short paragraphs — often 1-2 sentences each.
- Bullet lists for: requirements, documents being shared, next steps, options.
- Parenthetical questions embedded mid-sentence: "(who would be handling the onboarding, would it be you, Jess?)"

## Cold Outreach Template (consistent pattern)
1. Who I am (1 sentence): "I'm a Senior BD Manager at LinkedIn Learning, where I focus on course licensing partnerships."
2. Why I'm reaching out (1-2 sentences): "I came across [X] and wanted to reach out."
3. Value prop (1 sentence): "We're expanding our [Y] library and [Z] looks like a strong fit for our 30M+ enterprise learners."
4. CTA (1 sentence): "I'd love to set up a brief exploratory call to discuss whether there's an opportunity..."
5. Redirect (1 sentence): "Would you be the right person to connect with, or is there someone else on your team I should reach out to?"
6. Sign-off: `Very best,\nBrian`

## Quick Reply Patterns
- Affirmative: "Sounds good — let me know if I can help in any way."
- Enthusiastic agreement: "Brilliant — look forward to [X]."
- Very short: "Brilliant, connect then."
- Scheduling: "Next week would be grand!" or just lists times with no framing.
- Apology/reschedule: "Ack, sorry to hear that! Let's connect on Monday/Tuesday if that works for you."

## Vocabulary Fingerprints
- "Brilliant" (not "great" or "awesome")
- "grand" (as in "that would be grand")
- "Ack" (informal acknowledgment/apology)
- "fantastic"
- "Let me know if you have any questions or want to hop on a quick call." (common closer)
- "Would you be the right person to connect with...?" (cold outreach redirect)
- Uses "viz." for "for visibility" when CC'ing someone

## What He Avoids
- "Hope this email finds you well" / "I hope you're doing well"
- "Please don't hesitate to reach out" — he says "let me know"
- "Best regards" — just "Best"
- "I am writing to inform you" — states the point directly
- Long paragraphs, passive voice, hedging language
- **Em dashes** — do not use em dashes (—) in any form. Use a comma, period, or restructure the sentence.
- Emojis (extremely rare — one instance in a casual internal welcome email)

## Internal Emails
- Even shorter. Often just a sentence or two.
- Forwarding with context: "FYI" or one-line summary of why it matters.
- Meeting invites: very brief agenda in bullet points or one line.
- @-mentioning colleagues inline: "@Matt Fishbach would this fall under your purview?"
- Status updates: structured bullets with emoji section headers (newsletter format for team-wide updates only).
